Baby Simulator Tips & Strategies — Parenting Styles Guide

Last updated: 2025  ·  Reading time: ~7 minutes  ·  Baby Simulator Guide

Whether you're on your first playthrough or your tenth, Baby Simulator rewards players who understand how their decisions stack up over time. This guide covers the four main parenting philosophies, financial strategy, how to shape your child's traits, and how to approach the game's many possible endings.

The Four Parenting Styles

Child development research describes four broad parenting styles, and Baby Simulator reflects all of them through the types of choices you make. Understanding each style helps you play more intentionally — or to deliberately experiment with styles you wouldn't normally choose.

Authoritative

High warmth, high structure. You set clear rules but explain your reasoning and stay emotionally connected.

Tends toward: confident, empathetic, academically successful children

Authoritarian

Low warmth, high structure. Strict discipline with little room for the child's input or emotions.

Tends toward: disciplined but sometimes anxious or rebellious children

Permissive

High warmth, low structure. Loving and supportive, but few boundaries or consistent expectations.

Tends toward: creative and sociable but sometimes impulsive children

Uninvolved

Low warmth, low structure. Distant parenting, minimal involvement in the child's day-to-day life.

Tends toward: independent but potentially insecure children with complex outcomes

Pro tip: Mixing styles based on the situation often produces the most nuanced and realistic endings. A strict academic approach during school years combined with warmth during emotional crises mirrors how many real families operate.

Financial Strategy

Your family's wealth level affects which choices are available to you at each stage. However, starting wealthy doesn't guarantee a "better" ending — it simply opens different doors.

Playing as a Working-Class Family

Financial constraints create interesting constraints that shape your child's character. Children raised in working-class households in the game often develop stronger work ethic, community bonds, and resilience. You won't be able to afford every extracurricular or private tutor, so choices matter more. A scholarship opportunity, for example, becomes a major narrative moment rather than just one option among many.

Playing as a Middle-Class Family

The most flexible starting point. You can afford a range of activities and interventions without the unlimited budget of a wealthy family. Decisions carry real weight because you can't do everything — you have to prioritize.

Playing as a Wealthy Family

Money removes some constraints but introduces new ones: expectations, peer pressure, and the challenge of raising a grounded child in a privileged environment. The game raises interesting questions about whether money can substitute for time and emotional availability.

How to Shape Specific Traits

If you want to raise a child with particular strengths, here's how to direct your choices:

Raising a Creative Child

  • Prioritize art, music, and imaginative play in early childhood
  • Allow free time and unstructured exploration over rigid schedules
  • Support unconventional interests even when they seem impractical
  • Use the custom response option to encourage creative problem-solving in scenarios

Raising an Academically Strong Child

  • Build early reading habits and praise curiosity and effort over results
  • Choose educational activities and tutoring support when available
  • Address school conflicts with empathy but maintain academic standards
  • Be mindful of burnout in the teenage years — the game often presents scenarios where easing pressure preserves long-term performance

Raising a Socially Confident Child

  • Prioritize peer interaction and social activities, especially in early school years
  • Handle friendship conflicts with emotional coaching rather than just solving problems for them
  • Allow your child to take social risks and make their own mistakes
  • Model open communication in family relationships

Using the Custom Response Option

One of Baby Simulator's most powerful features is the ability to type your own parenting response instead of selecting from the preset choices. Many players overlook this option, but it consistently produces the most personalized and emotionally resonant outcomes.

The AI reads your custom input and interprets your intent, then generates a unique narrative outcome. A thoughtful custom response — one that considers your child's specific personality and the context of the scenario — often leads to richer story moments than any of the preset options.

Example: If your teenager comes home upset about a conflict at school and the preset options feel too blunt or too passive, try typing something like: "I sit down with them, make some tea, and just listen without trying to fix anything at first." The AI will create a nuanced outcome based on your specific approach.

Replay Value: Different Runs to Try

Baby Simulator is designed to be replayed. Here are some approaches that create very different experiences:

  1. The single parent run: Choose the single parent option and experience how different the financial and emotional calculations become
  2. Opposite gender/wealth: Try wealth levels and child genders you haven't used — the scenarios shift meaningfully
  3. Completely permissive run: Say yes to almost everything and watch where freedom without structure leads
  4. No custom options run: Restrict yourself to the preset choices only and see how the game's design guides narrative
  5. The tiger parent run: Push academics and structured achievement hard from the start — the teenage years often push back in surprising ways

Key Decisions That Have Outsized Impact

While every decision contributes, these moments tend to have the strongest influence on your child's final profile:

  • Ages 0–3: Attachment style decisions — how responsive and present you are — form the emotional foundation for everything that follows
  • Ages 5–7: School start and early learning approach shapes academic identity
  • Ages 10–12: Extracurricular choices and peer handling build social confidence
  • Ages 13–15: How you handle the first major teenage conflicts defines the trust relationship in late adolescence
  • Ages 16–18: The independence versus guidance balance in the final years directly shapes the ending character summary
